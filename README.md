Hyperbolic Mixed Membership Stochastic Blockmodel (H-MMSB): Hierarchical Community Detection Using Riemannian Geometry

SUMMARY
Real-world networks such as citation graphs, e-commerce platforms, and social networks exhibit inherent hierarchical community structures that conventional Euclidean models fundamentally fail to capture due to their polynomial volume growth. This thesis introduces the Hyperbolic Mixed Membership Stochastic Blockmodel (H-MMSB), a novel probabilistic graphical model that extends the classical Mixed Membership Stochastic Blockmodel from Euclidean space to the Lorentz hyperboloid, enabling natural representation of tree-like hierarchical communities through exponential volume growth. The proposed model places community centers directly on the hyperbolic manifold and computes node positions via the Fréchet mean with respect to learned mixed membership distributions, allowing each node to belong to multiple overlapping communities. Edge probabilities are modeled as a sigmoid function of hyperbolic geodesic distance, providing an interpretable geometric framework where radial displacement from the origin naturally encodes hierarchical depth while angular separation captures community affiliation. The model is trained using Riemannian Adam optimization with an evidence lower bound objective incorporating a novel hierarchical coherence penalty that encourages tree-like community arrangements. Comprehensive experiments were conducted on three real-world datasets namely Cora, CiteSeer, and Amazon co-purchasing network, comparing H-MMSB against four baseline models including Euclidean MMSB, Latent Space Model, Node2Vec, and SimpleGNN. Experimental results demonstrate that H-MMSB consistently and significantly outperforms all baseline models across all datasets, achieving AUC-ROC improvements ranging from 5% to 15% over Euclidean MMSB with the largest gains observed on the most hierarchically structured datasets. Ablation studies reveal that hyperbolic geometry contributes approximately 60% of the total performance improvement, mixed membership modeling contributes 30%, and hierarchical regularization contributes the remaining 10%. The Poincaré disk visualization of learned embeddings provides intuitive interpretability, revealing natural community hierarchies without requiring pre-specified taxonomy depths. Power-law degree distribution analysis confirms that all three datasets exhibit hierarchical signatures that hyperbolic geometry captures naturally while Euclidean space requires artificial forcing. The results establish hyperbolic space as the natural geometric framework for hierarchical network analysis and demonstrate that geometric inductive bias significantly improves performance on community detection tasks. This work opens avenues for extending geometric approaches to dynamic networks, directed graphs, and large-scale applications where hierarchical structure is fundamental.
